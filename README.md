# ✨ Poet Guy

> A browser-based poem generator inspired by the magic of street poets and wandering thoughts.

## 🌟 Highlights

- Interactive poem generator that responds to user input (word, thought, or feeling)
- Vintage typewriter animation with realistic typing delays
- Uses the Poemist API to fetch poems based on themes
- Retro aesthetic with vintage paper textures
- Single-page experience — no installation required

## 📖 Overview

Poet Guy is a browser-based poem generator inspired by the magic of street poets and the wandering thoughts we all carry. The project grew out of an encounter with @peoplearepoetry in Union Square, where I was drawn not only to the poems themselves, but to the slow, intentional process of someone listening, thinking, and typing something just for you.

What fascinated me was not just the poems, but the process: how he would ask for a theme or feeling, sometimes offering his own question if you were not sure. People could respond with anything, something specific or something abstract, and he would begin typing slowly and with intention. What made him unforgettable was the way he explained each poem after finishing. From that experience, I wanted to create an online version of this poetic encounter, a space where people might begin to love poetry the way I did.

## 🎨 Visual Direction

Visually, the project centers around the old typewriter to reference history, intimacy, and the feeling of someone writing something just for you. The interface carries the texture of vintage paper and the quiet presence of a page waiting for thought.

The sketch stage helped define how the project would feel before it was coded. Rather than treating the page as a neutral website layout, it was designed more like a poetic space, where the typewriter, paper, and pacing all contribute to the emotional tone of the experience.

## 🎮 Interaction Design

The interaction begins with a single open-ended prompt: "What's in your mind lately?" The participant enters a word, thought, or feeling, and that input acts like a divination, triggering a poem that relates to it either thematically or abstractly.

As the poem appears, each word is typed slowly on screen through a vintage typewriter animation, with small pauses that mimic a poet stopping to think. If the match feels random, that is part of the point. Like any good divination, it invites reflection rather than perfect relevance.
